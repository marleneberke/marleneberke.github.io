Research

My research uses computational modeling and behavioral experiments to understand how people reason about other minds. I'm especially interested in the mechanisms underlying theory of mind — how we infer what others are thinking, feeling, and experiencing — and what happens when these mechanisms are deployed in high-stakes real-world contexts like clinical care.

1. Theory of mind & cognitive processes

How do people infer the unobservable contents of other minds — what others are thinking, knowing, or experiencing? I build computational models of these inferences, grounding them in Bayesian inverse reasoning and testing them behaviorally.

2. ToM in communication

Theory of mind is not just for passive observation — it shapes how we speak, deceive, trust, and attend. This line examines how people strategically deploy mental-state reasoning in communicative and social contexts, from lying to audience modeling to child development.

3. ToM in the wild: pain, bodies & medicine

How do people reason about others' pain and bodily states? Deploying ToM in clinical and social contexts reveals systematic biases and failures — with direct consequences for how pain is believed, dismissed, and treated across patient groups.

4. Comparative cognition

What representations does a system need for intelligent behavior?

A parallel thread uses evolutionary simulations, non-human animals, and artificial systems as mirrors for human cognition — probing what computational machinery is necessary and sufficient to produce human-like reasoning about minds, objects, and the physical world.
